2. Simulation of Back-to-back Construction of Double Sheets

    We used oxDNA to simulate whether a back-to-back structure is actually achieved.
    Two important parameters in the simulation to determine whether a back-to-back structure can be achieved are the number of pieces to be joined and the size of each adhesive area. (Fig.12)
    In the first model, the four corners of the sheet were tested with eight bases of adhesive ends. (Fig.13) In this model, the adhesive ends were quickly removed and a stable two-piece structure could not be obtained. (Mov.1)
    Next, the four corners of the sheet were tested with two staple strands at each of the eight nucleotide adhesive ends.(Fig.14) Similar to the previous model, no stable bipartite structure was observed in this model. (Mov.2)
    In the next model, all staple ends of the sheet ends were used for adhesion.(Fig.15) In this model, the stable bipartite structure lasted longer than in the previous models, but eventually collapsed, which was not the result we were looking for.
    In the last model, the adhesive region of the previous model was extended from 8 bases to 16 bases to enhance the adhesive strength.(Fig. 16) In this model, the staple, which was unable to withstand the stress and pulled out earlier, was able to maintain hybridization, and the bipartite structure was stabilized. EXPERIMENT

Introduction

    The focus of this project is the creation of a single rolled sheet structure. We experimentally demonstrate the feasibility of achieving a rolled sheet structure through the designed bending energy.
    To prove that the sheet is indeed rolled, we created both an unfolded sheet structure, where the staple strands that form the central part of the sheet are removed, and a rolled sheet structure with all staple strands in place, then compared their sizes.

Annealing

    The salt concentration and temperature conditions important for folding were determined based on the original paper [1] and empirical data. AFM and QSEP were performed to observe the annealing results.
    The annealing solutions and annealing times were adjusted according to the table below.

AFM

    In atomic force microscopy, salt concentration and incubation time are important to successfully place the sample on the mica substrate. The incubation time was 30 minutes, and the magnesium ion concentration was compared between 12.5 mM and 18 mM, with the 18 mM sample having the clearer structure.
    The sample obtained by creating an open sheet structure showed a gourd-shaped structure as shown in the figure. The closed-sheet sample showed a spherical structure. This structure is lower in height than the closed-sheet structure we designed, and is more spread out horizontally. This is thought to be due to the interaction between mica and origami, which caused the closed sheet structure to collapse as shown in the following figure.
